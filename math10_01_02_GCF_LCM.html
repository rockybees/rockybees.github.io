Factors

An integer $k$ is called a $\textbf{factor}$ (or $\textbf{divisor}$) of an integer $N$ if there exists an interger $n$ such that $N = k \cdot n$. In other words, $k$ is a factor of $N$ if $N$ is divisible by $k$ without leaving a remainder. E.g., 4 is a factor of 24 because $4 \times 6 = 24$.

Divisibility Rules:

For $2$: A whole number is divisible by $2$ if its last digit is $0$, $2$, $4$, $6$, or $8$.

For $3$: A whole number is divisible by $3$ if the sum of its digits is divisible by $3$.

For $4$: A whole number is divisible by $4$ if the number formed by its last two digits is divisible by $4$.

For $5$: A whole number is divisible by $5$ if its last digit is $0$ or $5$.

For $6$: A whole number is divisible by $6$ if it is divisible by $2$ and $3$.

For $7$: A whole number is divisible by $7$ if doubling its last digit and subtracting it from the rest of the number results in a multiple of $7$.

For $8$: A whole number is divisible by $8$ if the number formed by its last three digits is divisible by $8$.

For $9$: A whole number is divisible by $9$ if the sum of its digits is divisible by $9$.

For $10$: A whole number is divisible by $10$ if its last digit is $0$.


One, Prime Numbers and Composite Numbers:

A $\textbf{prime number}$ is a natural number with exactly two distinct factors: $1$ and itself.

A $\textbf{composite number}$ is a natural number that has a factor other than $1$ or itself.

$1$ is neither prime nor composite.

Greatest Common Factor (GCF):

The $\textbf{Greatest Common Factor (GCF)}$ of two or more integers is the largest positive integer that divides each of the given numbers exactly without leaving a remainder.

To find the GCF of two or more numbers:

Method 1: Prime Factorization

  1. Perform prime factorization for each number.
  2. Identify the common prime factors and take the lowest exponent for each.
  3. Multiply these common factors to get the GCF.

Method 2: Continuous Division

  1. Divide the common factor of all the numbers until there is no common factor.
  2. Multiply these common factors to get the GCF.

Example 1:

Find the GCF of 12 and 16.

Method 1: Prime Factorization

$12 = 2^2 \times 3$

$16 = 2^4$

Therefore the GCF of $12$ and $16$ is $2^2 = 4$.

Method 2: Continuous Division


Example 2:

Find the GCF of 4, 6, and 16.

Method 1: Prime Factorization

$4 = 2^2$

$6 = 2 \times 3$

$16 = 2^4$

Therefore the GCF of $4$, $6$, and $16$ is $2$.

Method 2: Continuous Division


Least Common Multiple (LCM):

A multiple of a number is the product of that number and an integer. In other words, a number $M$ is a multiple of $N$ if there exists an integer $k$ such that $M = kN$. E.g., the multiples of $3$ are: ..., $-3$, $0$, $3$, $6$, $9$, ...

The $\textbf{Least Common Multiple (LCM)}$ of two or more integers is the smallest positive integer that is a multiple of each of the given numbers.

To find the LCM of two or more numbers:

Method 1: Prime Factorization

  1. Perform prime factorization for each number.
  2. Identify all distinct prime factors and take the highest exponent for each.
  3. Multiply all these factors to get the LCM.

Method 2: Continuous Division

  1. Divide the common factor of all the numbers or (any two numbers) until no common factor remains or all ones.
  2. Multiply all the factors, including the numbers that could not be divided, to get the LCM.

Example 3:

Find the LCM of 4, 6, and 16.

Method 1: Prime Factorization

$4 = 2^2$

$6 = 2 \times 3$

$16 = 2^4$

Therefore the GCF of $4$, $6$, and $16$ is $2^2 \times 3 \times 4 = 48$.

Method 2: Continuous Division


Example 4:

Find the LCM of 2, 3, and 6.

Method 1: Prime Factorization

$2 = 2$

$3 = 3$

$6 = 2 \times 3$

Therefore the LCM of $2$, $3$, and $6$ is $2 \times 3 = 6$.

Method 2: Continuous Division

Polynomials

A $\textbf{polynomial}$ is a specific typle of algebraic expression where the variables have $\textbf{whole number exponents}$ and appear only in the $\textbf{numerator}$ of each term, such as $2$,$\sqrt{5}x$, $x + y^2$ are polynomials, while $x^{1/2}$, $\frac{1}{x} - 2x$ are non-polynomials.

A polynomial with one term is called monomial, such as $3$, $2x^3y$, $-5y$.

A polynomial with two terms is called binomial, such as $ 2x+3$, $2x^3y - x$, $1-5y$.

A polynomial with three terms is called trinomial, such as $\sqrt{5}x^2 + 2x + 1$, $y^3 + a - 1$.

  • The $\textbf{degree}$ of a polynomial $\textbf{term}$ is the sum of the exponents of all its variables.
  • The term with the highest degree is called the $\textbf{leading term}$.
  • $\textbf{Like terms}$ are either constant terms or terms with the same variables raised to the same exponents.
  • The $\textbf{degree}$ of a $\textbf{polynomial}$ is the highest degree among its terms, i.e., the degree of the leading term.
Exponent Rules Summary Mathematical Form
Product Rule \( a^m \times a^n = a^{m+n} \)
Quotient Rule \( \frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}, a \neq 0 \)
Power Rule \( (a^m)^n = a^{m \times n} \)
Zero Exponent Rule $ a^0 = \frac{a^m}{a^m} = 1$, for any $a \neq 0$
Negative Exponent Rule \( a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n} \)
Fractional Exponent Rule $ a^{\frac{m}{n}} = \sqrt[n]{a^m} $

Note: For exponents of $0$, $0^0$ is undefined or indeterminate because different mathematical contexts suggest different values.

If we apply the above exponent rule to $0$, it would suggest $0^0 = 1$.

However, if we extend the exponent rule $0^n = 0$ (for any $n>0$) to $n = 0$, it would imply $0^0 = 0$, which contradicts the first rule.


Example:

Simplify $\sqrt[4]{16^2}$

Solution:

$\sqrt[4]{16^2} = \sqrt[4]{((2)^4)^2} = \sqrt[4]{2^8} = 2^(8/4) = 2^2 = 4$
